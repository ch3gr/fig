inFinite Image Generator

Two dimensional images of any kind, have long been stored and displayed digitally. To do so, the image is broken down into a set of pixels, with a pre-defined color depth. The digital representation of an image is essentially just a series of color values.

If we look at this the other way, we can say that any given digital canvas has a certain number of possible color combinations among its pixels. This number is calculated by raising the amount of available colors to the power of total pixels in the image [combinations=colorDepth^pixels]. So for example a tiny image with a resolution of 4x4 pixels and only 2 colors has 2^16=65536 possible color combinations.

Since any kind of image can be digitized, we could also assume that every single representation can be found in one of these possible color combinations, as long as the canvas has enough resolution and color definition to make the image readable. The above equation is exponential, so an image with adequate definition may have an astronomical number of possible combinations, but never the less a finite amount.

This experimental software exploits the artifact of digital imaging, where an algorithm samples an infinite reality into a finite data-set and attempts to explore this vast universe of color combinations within a digital canvas. The software iterates through all the possible combinations one by one, in an attempt ti generate every possible image. Even though the grand majority of the results may be nondescript, it is guaranteed that eventually meaningful images will emerge. These images could be of anything, like a photo of your cat, your own portrait, the photo of your unborn child, or even a mathematical equation that hasn't been invented yet. Essentially it is the ultimate digital image creation tool.

Of course at the moment this only works in a theoretical level, as in practice, even when running at 60 iterations per second, it would require a little less 10 billion years, or almost the age of the known universe, to iterate through all the possible color combinations of an 8x8 pixels image with only 2 colors. But as the processing power of computers still keeps doubling every two years based on Moore's law and with the fast progress in quantum computing, there may be a time in the future when we will be able to brute force and filter all the meaningful images out of the chaos. In a potential version 2 of this program, the output images can be passed into an image recognition algorithm, which will identify, aggregate and categorize human recognizable results.

So what happens when and if we get to that point? Will this be the end of human creativity? What about intellectual property? Who will own the rights to all the images?

Q&A: Q. Why don't I see any meaningful images when I move the slider? A. The slider's accuracy is limited by the screen resolution. Moving the slider just by one pixel, may translate to an increment of many trillion iterations, which means it's easy to miss some. Q. Why are there only grey colors? A. Colors require 3 values per pixel, so for the purpose of keeping this experiment simple, only tones of grey are used. The logic can easily be transferred into color images, or even moving images.
